So! How do we do math exactly? 
It seems intuitive, but if we want to build a machine to do it for us (aka, a computer), we need to know specifically what's going on.
Thankfully math can be boiled down to a few simple principles, and we'll see those in action 'here' (point to blackboard) as we solve this (2 + 3 = ?)
~
First, we need some way of representing our numbers and a place to put them. (highlight '2', '3' and '?')
In our case, it'll be these spots here (underscore '2', '3' and '?' on the blackboard) 
And our choice of representation is common-numerals, even though there are many other options (show Roman numerals, Chinese, Hebrew, and simply dots) 
Let's call all this "Memory". (Show on Blackboard)

Next we need a way of doing things with our numbers, like mathematical operations.
Right now we're doing addition, but also we need a way move our numbers around, like "placing" our answer 'here' (highlight the '?').
Traditionally this is called the "logic" because each step is accurate and, well, logical.
However, we're gonna call it "Processing" to keep with the context. (Show on Blackboard)

Third, and this may seem subtle, but we need a way to interact with our numbers. 
An ability to write them down and read them out.
Right now, in our case, we're using this "blackboard". But it doesn't matter what medium it is, so long as we can interact with it.
We'll call that an "Interface". (Show on Blackboard)

And lastly we need some way to move from one step to the next.
Our example is pretty small, but math problems can grow pretty large (show some calculus and what not) So having a reliable way of progressing is important.
We'll call that "Automation". (Show on Blackboard)

And that's it! That's more or less how math works.
And the whole process of doing math, using logical steps to solve a problem, that's called following an "Algorithm". (Show on Blackboard)
